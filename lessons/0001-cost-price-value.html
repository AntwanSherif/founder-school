Cost, Price, Value

Three numbers engineers tend to collapse into one. Pulling them apart is the first real money skill — and it's where almost all your pricing power hides.

Here's the trap, and it's set specifically for people like you. You built EncoreShot. You know exactly what it costs to score one user's photos — the Gemini call, the storage, the bandwidth. Call it $0.15 a month for a hobbyist. So the instinct is clean and logical: take the cost, add a sensible margin, that's the price. Cost plus markup. Done.

That instinct will quietly cap your business. Because cost, price, and value are three different numbers, and the one you understand best — cost — is the one that matters least for setting your price.

The pricing thermometer

This is Kevin Hale's model from Y Combinator (your primary source below). Picture a thermometer with three marks stacked from bottom to top:

VALUE
PRICE
COST
Value — what the result is worth to the customer.
↕ the gap here is their delight — the reason to buy & stay.
Price — the number you choose to charge.
↕ the gap here is your margin — fuel to grow.
Cost — what it costs you to deliver one more.

Two gaps, two jobs. Price minus cost is your margin — the money that pays for everything else you'll ever do. Value minus price is the customer's delight — the surplus they pocket, which is exactly why they buy and why they don't churn. Good pricing is the deliberate act of sliding "price" up the bar until both gaps are healthy.

Notice what cost does here: it sets the floor. Price can't sit below it for long or you lose money on every sale. But the floor doesn't tell you where the ceiling is — and the ceiling is set by value, which has nothing to do with your compute bill.

EncoreShot, concretely Your cost floor is ~$0.15/user/mo. But the value? It's the one Story that finally does the show justice, posted inside the same-night window before the moment's gone — and the two hours of squinting-at-a-camera-roll you just handed back. Nobody at a Bad Bunny show is doing cost-plus math. They're weighing "$5" against "the night I'll never shoot again." That's the number your price negotiates with — not the $0.15.

Why cost-plus is the engineer's tax

When you price off cost, you let your own efficiency punish you. Make the AI 10× cheaper and cost-plus says lower the price — even though the value to the fan didn't drop a cent. You'd be donating your engineering wins to your customers instead of keeping them as margin. Value-based pricing — Hale's recommendation — anchors instead on what the outcome is worth. His rough target: aim for perceived value around 10× the price, so the buy feels like an obvious yes.

"The most common pricing mistakes: underestimating costs, not understanding your value, charging the wrong customers, and undercharging." — Kevin Hale, YC

And his most quietly radical rule of thumb: most startups charge too little, so keep raising price until you start losing about 20% of customers. Until that happens, you haven't found the edge of what people will pay — you've just been leaving money (and the signal it carries) on the table.

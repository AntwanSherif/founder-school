Founder School · Lesson 03 · Pricing

Models & packaging

You know the floor and the ceiling. Now the shape: how you charge, and how you arrange what people get for the money. This is where most of your revenue is actually won or lost.

There's a quiet truth in pricing: how you charge often matters more than how much. The same product at "$5/mo," "$0.10/asset," or "$49 once" attracts different people, churns differently, and feels different — before you've changed a single number. Let's build the vocabulary, then apply it to EncoreShot's open questions.

The three pricing models

ModelYou charge by…Fits when…
Subscriptiontime (per month/year)usage is steady & the product is a habit
Usage-basedwhat they consume (assets, exports)usage is spiky and tracks the value/cost
One-time / lifetimeonce, up frontbuyers are occasional; recurring feels unfair

Most real companies blend these — nearly half of SaaS run two or three at once (Monetizely, 2026). The choice isn't religious; it's about matching how the customer experiences value.

EncoreShot — the model fit Your hobbyist is the textbook occasional buyer: a few shows a year, no hard deadline, WTP $0–5/mo. A monthly subscription invites them to cancel between concerts. That's exactly why your pricing brief floats a one-time / lifetime "Founding" license — it captures the occasional shooter who'd churn out of a subscription. The Pro persona, who shoots weekly under deadline, is where subscription earns its keep later. Different personas may want different models — that's allowed.
2026-06-23 update — the persona that actually converts The "$0–5/mo" range above applies to casual fans — and MIDiA Research (2025) puts it even lower: roughly 23% of music fans express any willingness to pay for AI editing tools, and freemium-to-paid conversion for casual consumers runs 1–5%. The prosumer (superfan shooting multiple shows a month, identity tied to posting quality) converts at >20%. The Founding license is the right model; the persona who buys and retains it is likely the prosumer, not the one-show-a-year hobbyist. Calibrate the "aha moment" and the free-tier gate accordingly. The value metric: what your meter measures

A value metric is the unit you charge by — Slack charges per active user, Dropbox per GB, Twilio per message. The golden rule: the metric should rise with the value the customer gets (so paying more never feels like a punishment), and ideally track your cost too. Pick wrong and growth fights you.

For EncoreShot the candidates are shows/sessions, assets scored, or exports. Note the trap: "assets scored" tracks your AI cost beautifully but is a terrible customer meter — nobody wants a bill that grows because they shot more frames. Exports (or just unlimited under a flat tier) tracks their value — the keeper they posted — far better.

Good-Better-Best: let people sort themselves

The most reliable packaging on earth is three tiers (Rafi Mohammed, HBR). It works because customers self-select by willingness to pay, and the mere presence of a premium tier makes the middle look reasonable (the "anchor"). What separates the tiers is a price fence — a feature or limit that gently stops a high-value user from buying the cheap plan.

Designing the free tier (your hardest open question)

A free tier is a marketing cost, not charity — its job is to deliver the "aha moment" and then create a reason to pay. The cardinal rule from your own notes: the free constraint must let the user see export quality on their own content before paying, or the trust mechanism breaks. So the gate should sit after the magic, not before it. Gate on quantity (1 show, N exports) — never on quality (don't ship a worse export to free users; that poisons the proof).

"Set the perceived value at ~10× the price; then practise raising prices until you lose about 20% of customers." — Kevin Hale, YC (Lesson 01)

Your one win from this lesson You can now reason about EncoreShot's pricing as three separable decisions — model (lifetime for the occasional fan), value metric (charge by exports/shows, not assets-scored), and packaging (good-better-best with a quantity gate that fires after the aha moment). That turns "what do I charge?" from one scary question into three answerable ones.

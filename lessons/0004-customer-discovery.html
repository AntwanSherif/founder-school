Talking to users without fooling yourself

Everything so far — value, floor, tiers — rests on one input: knowing what your users actually need and would pay for. The catch is that people lie to you, kindly, by default.

When you show a fan EncoreShot at a concert and they say "oh that's cool, I'd totally use that," your brain files it as validation. It isn't. It's politeness. The single most expensive mistake an engineer-founder makes is building confidently on compliments. The fix is a discipline called The Mom Test — named because its questions are so well-formed that even your mom couldn't lie to you about your idea (Rob Fitzpatrick; YC recommends it to every founder).

The three rules

  • Talk about their life, not your idea. The moment you describe your product, they start managing your feelings. Keep your idea in your pocket and ask about them.
  • Ask about specifics in the past, not opinions about the future. "Would you use X?" invites a fantasy. "Walk me through the last show you shot" gets you facts.
  • Talk less, listen more. You're there to learn, not to pitch. If you're talking, you're not learning.

The tell: facts vs. fluff

Sort everything you hear into two bins. Facts = what they actually did, what it cost them, what they've already tried, what they currently pay for. Fluff = compliments, generic claims ("I always…"), and hypotheticals about the future. Fluff feels great and means nothing. Only facts should move your roadmap or your price.

Fluff (don't trust)

"Do you like this app?"

"Would you pay $5 for it?"

"Would you use AI to cull?"

Facts (trust these)

"Walk me through the last show you shot — then what?"

"How long did editing those clips take you?"

"What do you use today, and what do you pay for it?"

EncoreShot — your built-in advantage Your GTM already is customer discovery. The concert-seeding script — "Did you get any good shots?", riff on the show, empathise with the pain — is textbook Mom Test: it opens on their life, not your pitch. Lean into it. At every show, before you ever mention EncoreShot, learn three facts: how many clips they shot, whether they posted that night, and what stopped them. That's free, high-signal research your competitors can't buy — and it's the raw data behind every pricing and feature call you'll make.

The commitment test

Talk is cheap; commitment isn't. The strongest signal isn't a "yes," it's a person giving up something they value — time, reputation, or money. A redeemed voucher that turns into a first export is worth more than a hundred "love it"s. That's why your funnel measures landed → signed up → first session → first export: each step is a real commitment, not a compliment.

"Opinions are worthless. … Anything involving the future is an over-optimistic lie." — Rob Fitzpatrick, The Mom Test

Check yourself

Three questions

1. The Mom Test says talk about…

2. Which question passes the Mom Test?

3. Compliments and "I'd totally buy it" are…

Your one win from this lesson Next concert, you'll run three Mom-Test questions before pitching anything — and you'll sort every answer into facts vs. fluff, trusting only the facts. You've turned "people seemed to like it" (worthless) into a repeatable way to extract truth from the exact humans you're building for.

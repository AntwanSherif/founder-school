Founder School · Lesson 07 · Metrics

The few numbers a solo founder watches

You can measure everything. You should measure almost nothing. The skill is knowing which handful of numbers actually steer the ship — and ignoring the flattering rest.

As an engineer you can instrument anything, and that's the danger: dashboards full of numbers that feel like progress and change nothing. Dave McClure named the antidote in 2007 — Pirate Metrics, or AARRR, the only five numbers that matter (Amplitude). Five stages of one customer's journey, each a question you can actually answer.

AARRR — the five stages

AAcquisition — how do people find you?voucher handed / landed
AActivation — do they hit the first "wow"?first export
RRetention — do they come back?return next show
RReferral — do they tell others?voucher shared
RRevenue — will they pay?paid conversion

Notice this is the same journey from Lesson 04's commitment ladder and Lesson 05's referral loop — AARRR just gives it a measurable name at each rung. Your GTM analytics floor already tracks most of it.

Vanity vs. actionable

A vanity metric looks impressive and guides no decision: total signups, page views, Instagram likes. An actionable metric ties to a choice you'd make differently. The test: "if this number doubled tomorrow, what would I do?" If the answer is "feel good," it's vanity. "Total vouchers handed out" is vanity; "what % of handed vouchers reach a first export" tells you whether your funnel leaks — and where.

EncoreShot — pick your one North Star A solo founder can't optimize five things at once. At your stage, the metric that best captures real value delivered is weekly/monthly first-export rate — fans who got an actual keeper out the door. It sits at Activation (proves the magic works) and feeds Retention (the ones who'll return). Acquisition without activation is just noise; revenue comes later. Make that one number the heartbeat of your Founding Crew updates.

One number, for now

The "North Star Metric" idea: choose the single number that best proxies the value customers get, and orient everything around it. It changes as you grow (activation now → retention later → revenue after pricing). The discipline is having one, not ten. Everything else is a supporting diagnostic you glance at only when the North Star moves.

"Focus on the metrics that directly affect the health of your business — ignore the superficial ones." — Dave McClure, Startup Metrics for Pirates

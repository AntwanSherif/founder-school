Product-market fit, and how you'd know

The most-used and least-understood phrase in startups. Most founders chase it like a vibe. There's a sharper way to see it — and you've already built the instrument that measures it.

Marc Andreessen, who coined the term, said you can always feel product-market fit (PMF) when it happens — usage outpaces what you can handle, the product gets pulled out of your hands. True, but useless when you're at 10 users and squinting for signal. So let's make it measurable. PMF is simply this: you've built something a specific group of people genuinely want and would miss. The trick is detecting it before the tidal wave.

The Sean Ellis test

The cleanest early proxy comes from growth investor Sean Ellis. Ask your active users one question:

"How would you feel if you could no longer use EncoreShot?"

  • Very disappointed
  • Somewhat disappointed
  • Not disappointed

Across ~100 startups, Ellis found a line: when 40% or more say "very disappointed," growth tends to come; below that, it usually stalls (First Round Review). It's a rough heuristic, not a law — but it converts a vibe into a number you can move.

Superhuman: turning the number into a roadmap

The email startup Superhuman scored just 22% at first — nowhere near fit. Instead of guessing, founder Rahul Vohra built an engine: segment the "very disappointed" users, learn what they love, find what holds the "somewhat" group back, and rebuild the roadmap around exactly that. Three quarters later: 58%. The lesson isn't the survey — it's that PMF is something you can systematically engineer, not just wait for.

EncoreShot — you already have the instrument Your voucher cohort funnel (landed → signed up → first session → first export → 7d return → 30d return) is a PMF detector in disguise. The Sean Ellis survey is a one-line addition to your Founding Crew chat. But your truest signal is buried in those last two steps: do fans come back for their next show? One delighted export is a nice moment; a fan who returns three concerts later is product-market fit you can feel.

Leading vs. lagging signals

A lagging signal (revenue, total users) confirms fit after the fact — real, but slow and easy to fake with a launch spike. A leading signal (the 40% score, the shape of your retention curve, organic word-of-mouth) tells you early, while you can still act. Chase leading signals. And the most honest leading signal of all is retention — which is exactly Lesson 08.

"The only thing that matters is getting to product-market fit. … When you are not at PMF, you can always feel its absence." — Marc Andreessen

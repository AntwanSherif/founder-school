Founder School · Lesson 05 · Distribution

Getting your first users

A great product nobody can find is a hobby. This lesson is about the other half of the business — how people discover you — and why it's the half engineers chronically neglect.

Here's the uncomfortable stat that should reframe your whole effort: poor distribution, not a bad product, is the #1 cause of startup failure (Gabriel Weinberg, Traction). Founders romanticise the build and assume good products spread on their own. They don't. You should spend roughly as much energy on getting users as on building for them — and the good news is you've already designed a great channel.

The Bullseye framework

Weinberg catalogs 19 traction channels (SEO, content, ads, viral/referral, events, PR, community, sales, partnerships…). The trap is dabbling in all of them. Bullseye says: at any given stage, one channel drives the bulk of your growth — your job is to find it cheaply, then pour in.

Outer ring — brainstormall 19 channels, no filtering
Middle ring — test3–4 cheap experiments
Bullseye — focusthe one channel that's working
  1. Brainstorm a plausible version of all 19 — even the silly ones.
  2. Rank into three columns: promising / maybe / long-shot.
  3. Test the top 3–4 with cheap, time-boxed experiments (the question is "can this work?", not "scale it").
  4. Focus everything on the one that shows traction; revisit when it saturates.
EncoreShot — you already found your bullseye Your GTM playbook isn't dabbling — it's a correctly-chosen core channel: in-person concert seeding (offline events + community), zero-CAC by design. That's the right call for 0–25 users. The Bullseye discipline now is to resist spreading thin (don't start running ads at 10 users) — and to treat your other listed channels (fan-group seeding, micro-creators, build-in-public) as the middle-ring tests you graduate one at a time once seeding saturates. Cohort data per voucher is exactly how you'll know which test earned promotion.

"Do things that don't scale" — on purpose

At your stage, the un-scalable is the strategy. Handing a QR sticker to a fan at 2am doesn't scale — and that's fine, because it builds something ads can't: a real relationship and a true story (Paul Graham, "Do Things That Don't Scale"). The first 25 users should feel recruited by a person, not acquired by a funnel. Your Founding Crew chat is this principle in product form.

Referrals: turn users into the channel

Once people get value, the cheapest growth is letting them bring the next person. Referred users convert 3–5× more often and stay longer (Refgrow, 2026). What the data says works:

PrincipleWhy
Two-sided reward (giver and receiver)+85% participation vs one-sided
Trigger after the "aha moment"+60% participation — ask right after a first export, never before
Credit / perks beat cash~18% better in software; cheaper for you, on-brand
Kill the frictionevery extra click sheds referrers

Referral vs affiliate — worth keeping straight: a referral is a happy user sharing for a perk (your voucher flywheel — extend it: "send a voucher, you both get X"). An affiliate is an outsider (a creator/blogger) paid commission to promote you — that's a later, Pro-phase lever, not a 0–25-user move.

"The number one reason we pass on entrepreneurs is they're focused on the product instead of distribution." — common VC refrain echoing Traction's thesis.

Glossary

The vocabulary, defined once and reused everywhere. New terms land here as lessons introduce them. EncoreShot-specific notes in rust.

Cost (COGS)
What it costs you to deliver the product to one more customer — "Cost of Goods Sold." For software this is mostly infrastructure + per-use compute. For EncoreShot, the dominant cost is the AI scoring call per asset (driven by video duration, resolution, fps, model) + storage/bandwidth — ~$0.15/user/mo for a hobbyist.
Price
The number on the tag — what the customer actually pays. A decision you make, not a fact derived from cost.
Value
What the outcome is worth to the customer — measured in money, time saved, status, or pain removed. Usually far higher than price, and unrelated to your cost. For EncoreShot: the one Story that does the show justice, posted inside the same-night window; hours of culling collapsed to minutes.
Pricing thermometer
Kevin Hale's (YC) mental model: stack cost (bottom), price (middle), value (top). The gap price − cost is your margin (fuel to grow); the gap value − price is the customer's delight (their reason to buy and stay). Pricing well means placing price in that band on purpose.
Cost-plus pricing
Setting price = cost + a markup. Feels safe and "fair," but ignores value entirely and usually leaves most of the money on the table. The classic engineer trap: "my AI costs $X/asset, so I'll charge $X + margin."
Value-based pricing
Setting price from what the outcome is worth to the customer, anchored against alternatives — not from your cost. Hale's rule of thumb: aim for perceived value ≈ 10× price.
Margin (gross margin)
The slice of each sale left after the cost of delivering it: (price − cost) ÷ price. Traditional SaaS runs 70–90%; AI products often run lower (20–60%) because compute per use is real and variable. The fuel that pays for everything else.
Willingness to pay (WTP)
The most a given customer would pay before walking away. Differs by segment. EncoreShot hobbyist WTP ≈ $0–5/mo or one-time; Pro ≈ $12–25/mo — same product, very different ceilings.
Unit economics
The profit/loss on a single unit (one customer, one transaction) once you strip away fixed costs. If the unit doesn't make sense, scaling just loses money faster. For EncoreShot the "unit" is one user-month: revenue vs. their AI + storage cost.
Value metric
The unit you charge by — per user, per GB, per export. The golden rule: it should rise with the value the customer gets (so paying more never feels like punishment), and ideally track your cost too. For EncoreShot, "exports" or "shows" is a better meter than "assets scored" — the latter tracks your cost but punishes the user for shooting more.
Price fence
A feature or limit that separates pricing tiers and gently stops a high-value customer from buying the cheap plan (e.g. "Free = 1 show"). The logic that makes good-better-best tiers hold instead of everyone trading down.
Aha moment
The instant a user first feels the product's core value. Everything in onboarding, free-tier design, and referral timing should aim to reach it fast — and ask for money / referrals only after it. For EncoreShot: seeing their own bad footage become a Story-ready keeper.
CAC
Customer Acquisition Cost — the average spend to win one customer. EncoreShot's wedge is "zero-CAC by design": in-person seeding costs time, not ad dollars.
Traction channel
One of the ~19 distinct ways to reach customers (events, SEO, content, referral, ads, PR, community…). The Bullseye framework says find the one that works at your stage and focus there, rather than dabbling in all of them.
The Mom Test
A discipline for customer interviews (Rob Fitzpatrick): talk about their life not your idea; ask about specific past behaviour not future opinions; talk less. Sort answers into facts (trust) vs fluff (ignore). Compliments are fluff.
Referral vs. affiliate
A referral is a happy user sharing for a perk (best: two-sided reward, triggered after the aha moment). An affiliate is an outsider paid commission to promote you — a later-stage lever, not an early-user move.
